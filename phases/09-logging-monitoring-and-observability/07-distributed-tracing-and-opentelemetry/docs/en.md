# Distributed Tracing & OpenTelemetry

> Metrics told you the 99th percentile exploded. Correlated logs let you pull every line for the request. You still cannot say **where the 4.2 seconds went**, because a log line is a point in time and latency is an *interval* — and the request touched eight services on machines whose clocks disagree. A trace is the missing shape: one request, drawn as nested intervals on one timeline. This lesson builds a tracer, renders a real waterfall, and makes the head-versus-tail sampling trade-off numeric.

**Type:** Build
**Languages:** Python
**Prerequisites:** [Correlation: Request IDs, Trace Context & Propagation](../03-correlation-and-request-context/), [Prometheus: Pull, Exposition & PromQL](../06-prometheus-and-promql/)
**Time:** ~90 minutes

## The Problem

It is 03:14 again. A checkout took **4.2 seconds**. Your instrumentation is genuinely good: the
Prometheus histogram from Lesson 6 told you `p99` for `/checkout` went from 180 ms to 4.3 s at
03:09, and the correlated logs from Lessons 2 and 3 let you pull every line the request emitted by
its `trace_id`. Here is that pull:

```json
{"ts":"03:14:07.001","service":"gateway","event":"checkout_start","trace_id":"51ea40b0…"}
{"ts":"03:14:07.043","service":"gateway","event":"db_query_done","trace_id":"51ea40b0…"}
{"ts":"03:14:06.998","service":"payment","event":"charge_received","trace_id":"51ea40b0…"}
{"ts":"03:14:11.190","service":"payment","event":"bank_authorized","trace_id":"51ea40b0…"}
{"ts":"03:14:11.200","service":"gateway","event":"checkout_done","trace_id":"51ea40b0…"}
```

Now subtract timestamps by hand to find where the time went, and hit two walls.

**Logs are points, not intervals.** `checkout_start` and `db_query_done` are 42 ms apart, but you
cannot tell whether the database call took 42 ms or 2 ms with 40 ms of queueing in front of it,
because nothing recorded when the call *began*. Every duration you want is the gap between two
events you may not have logged, and parallel work is indistinguishable from sequential work.

**The clocks are lying.** `charge_received` on the payment service is stamped `03:14:06.998` —
**three milliseconds before the gateway request that caused it started**. That is not time travel,
it is ordinary **clock skew**. Two machines running NTP (Network Time Protocol) typically sit within
a few milliseconds of each other and drift to tens or hundreds under load. The rule every distributed system lives by:
wall-clock timestamps from different machines cannot order events, and subtracting them across a
boundary yields a number with an unknown error bar. Your reconstruction is not merely miserable to
do by hand — it is **wrong**.

Notice what you gave up. When this was one process you had a **stack trace**: a free,
perfectly-ordered record of who called whom, generated by the runtime at no cost. Splitting the
monolith deleted it and nobody replaced it. A **distributed trace** is that replacement, rebuilt
deliberately — the same tree of calls, spanning processes, with real durations attached. The design
comes from Google's **Dapper** paper (Sigelman et al., 2010), and every tracing system you will use
is a descendant of it.

## The Concept

### The span: an interval, not a point

The atom of tracing is the **span**: one named operation with a **start and an end**. That single
change — interval instead of instant — is what makes tracing a different pillar rather than
"structured logs with extra steps".

Three fields deserve extra attention. **`name`** must be low cardinality — `GET /orders/{id}`,
never `GET /orders/8842` — for the same reason a Prometheus label must be (Phase 4, Lesson 5): a
backend groups spans by name, and a unique name per request makes grouping meaningless. **`events`**
are timestamped points *inside* the span, which is where a log line belongs when it describes a
moment rather than a duration; a recorded exception is just an event named `exception`. **`links`**
are causal edges that are not parent/child — a queue consumer processing a message an hour later
*links* to the producer's span rather than claiming it as a parent, because its work is not inside
the producer's interval (Phase 6's async world).

### Span kind, and why the backend cares

**Span kind** tells the backend what role the span played: **`SERVER`** (handling an inbound
request), **`CLIENT`** (making an outbound call and **blocked** until it returns), **`PRODUCER`**
(enqueued a message and did *not* wait), **`CONSUMER`** (took a message off a queue, later), and
**`INTERNAL`** (in-process work — the default, and the right kind for your business logic).

Kind is not decoration. A `CLIENT` span and the `SERVER` span it triggered are a matched pair
covering one call from both ends, and their difference is your **network and queueing time** — in
the run below, 4074.40 ms versus 4065.90 ms, so 8.50 ms went on the wire. Backends use kind to build
service-dependency graphs (`CLIENT` in A pointing at `SERVER` in B is an edge) and to know a
`PRODUCER`/`CONSUMER` pair must *not* be drawn as a blocking nested call.

### A trace is a tree; the UI is a waterfall

A **trace** is every span sharing one `trace_id`. Because each span names its parent, those spans
form a **tree** (strictly a DAG — directed acyclic graph — once links are counted). But nobody
debugs by reading a tree. The view you stare at is the **waterfall**: the same tree with each span
as a horizontal bar, positioned by start time and scaled by duration, on one shared axis.

Nesting becomes indentation, and a child's bar always sits inside its parent's, because a child's
interval is contained in its parent's. Four shapes cover almost every diagnosis you will make:

- **A long bar whose child is equally long** → the time is downstream; your service is just waiting.
- **A long bar with short children** → the time is in **your own code** between the calls: the JSON
  serialization, the template render, the tight loop, the lock.
- **Staggered children, each starting after the last ended** → **sequential** calls that were
  probably independent. Fan them out and the total collapses to the slowest one.
- **A gap before the first child** → the request sat somewhere before doing work: connection-pool
  wait, thread-pool queueing, lock acquisition, garbage collection.

### The critical path, and what only tracing shows

Not every span costs you latency. The **critical path** is the chain of spans that actually
determines total duration — the longest root-to-leaf path through spans that block each other.
Spans running *in parallel* with it are free until they grow longer than it. Above, the critical
path accounts for 4036 of the 4200 ms; the three database queries are real work, but optimizing
them saves at most 118 ms of a 4.2-second request. **Optimizing off the critical path is wasted
effort**, and the waterfall is the only view that shows which is which. Four findings belong to
tracing alone:

- **N+1 queries.** Twenty sibling spans all named `SELECT order_item` is the Phase 3 Lesson 14 N+1
  problem made visible. A metric shows one slow endpoint; a log shows one query; only the trace
  shows *two hundred of them in a row*.
- **Retry storms.** One logical call appearing as three `CLIENT` spans with exponential gaps — or
  one span carrying a `retry` event — says the latency is a retry policy, not a slow dependency.
- **Serial work that should be concurrent** — the staggered-children shape above.
- **Time spent waiting rather than working.** A 900 ms span with no children is a queue or a lock,
  not computation. Nothing else in your telemetry distinguishes those.

### Sampling: you cannot keep it all

One request emitting one log line emits **one** record. The same request emitting a span per
operation across eight services emits **thirty to eighty**, each larger than a log line. Tracing is
the most expensive pillar per request by an order of magnitude, so at real traffic you **sample**:
keep some traces in full and discard the rest in full. Never keep *part* of a trace — half a
waterfall is worse than none.

**Head-based sampling** decides at the root and propagates the answer as the `sampled` bit in the
`traceparent` header — the flags byte from Lesson 3. A **probabilistic** root sampler computes the
decision from the **trace ID itself** (`hash(trace_id) < ratio × 2⁶⁴`) rather than flipping a coin,
which makes it **consistent**: every service independently computes the *same* answer, so a trace is
never collected half-way. A **parent-based** sampler simply obeys the incoming header. Head sampling
costs essentially nothing — unsampled spans are never built. Its flaw is fundamental: **you decide
before you know whether the request was interesting**, so a 1% head sample keeps 1% of your errors.

**Tail-based sampling** moves the decision to the **collector**, which buffers every span of a trace
until it finishes (or a timeout fires), then applies a policy to the *completed* trace: keep all
errors, keep everything over p95 latency, keep 10% of the rest. The price is real: the collector
holds every in-flight trace in memory, and because it can only judge a trace it holds *entirely*,
**every span of a trace must reach the same collector instance** — which forces a load-balancing
exporter sharded on `trace_id`. You also pay full application and network cost for spans you discard.
The practical default: **keep 100% of errors and slow requests, sample the boring ones hard.**

### OpenTelemetry: API, SDK, exporters, Collector, OTLP

**OpenTelemetry** (OTel) is the standard for all three pillars, hosted by the CNCF (Cloud Native
Computing Foundation), and its architecture is a deliberate set of seams:

- **The API** (Application Programming Interface) is what your code calls — and it is a **no-op**
  unless an SDK is installed. That is why a *library* (an HTTP client, an ORM) can instrument itself
  against the API and add zero backend dependency for its users. Before this split, instrumenting
  meant importing a vendor's SDK, and switching vendors meant rewriting every instrumented line.
- **The SDK** (Software Development Kit — the implementation behind the API) is what you configure
  in the application: **samplers**, **span processors** (batching, queueing) and **resource
  detection** (filling in `service.name`, `k8s.pod.name`).
- **Exporters** serialize finished spans to a protocol: **OTLP** (OpenTelemetry Protocol — the
  native one, protobuf over gRPC or HTTP), or legacy Jaeger/Zipkin formats.
- **The Collector** is a *separate process* built from **receivers → processors → exporters**, where
  batching, attribute scrubbing, tail sampling and fan-out belong — so none of it lives in your app
  and none of it needs a redeploy to change.

### Semantic conventions, and auto versus manual

An attribute is only useful if everyone spells it the same. **Semantic conventions** are OTel's
registry of standard names: `http.request.method`, `http.response.status_code`, `url.path`,
`server.address`, `db.system.name`, `db.query.text`, `messaging.system`, plus the resource
attributes `service.name` and `service.version`. Use them and every out-of-the-box dashboard and
service map works immediately; call it `method` instead and you own those dashboards forever.

**Auto-instrumentation** monkey-patches the libraries you already use — web framework, HTTP client,
database driver, queue client — so every inbound request becomes a `SERVER` span, every outbound
call a `CLIENT` span, and `traceparent` is injected and extracted for you. That is 80% of the value
for zero code. What it cannot know is **your business logic**: it will never produce a span called
`score_fraud_risk`. So turn it on everywhere, then add a small number of manual `INTERNAL` spans
around operations you would actually want a bar for. A handful per service beats a span around every
function, which just makes the waterfall unreadable.

Finally, tracing is what closes Lesson 1's funnel — *a metric wakes you, a trace localizes the bad
hop, a log explains it*. A span's `trace_id` is the same `trace_id` you put on every log line in
Lesson 2, so a click on a span pulls its logs; and **exemplars** attach a `trace_id` to a specific
observation inside a Prometheus histogram bucket, so clicking the tall bar in the p99 graph lands
you in the trace that produced it.

## Build It

`code/mini_tracer.py` is a working tracer in standard-library Python: the span model, a
context-manager tracer, W3C propagation across a **real HTTP hop**, two exporters (one drawing a
waterfall), a head sampler and a tail-sampling simulator. Time is a fake microsecond clock advanced
by hand instead of `sleep` and the RNG is seeded, so every number below reproduces.

The tracer is one context manager. It reads the current span from a `ContextVar` to find its parent,
makes itself current for the block, records any exception as an event with `status=ERROR`, and on
exit stamps the end time and hands the span to the exporters — but only if the trace was sampled:

```python
@contextmanager
def start_span(self, name, kind=SpanKind.INTERNAL, attributes=None,
               links=None, remote_parent=None):
    parent = remote_parent
    if parent is None:                                    # else: the enclosing span
        here = _CURRENT.get()
        parent = here.context if here is not None else None
    trace_id = parent.trace_id if parent else _hex_id(16)
    sampled = self.sampler.should_sample(parent, trace_id)
    span = Span(name, SpanContext(trace_id, _hex_id(8), 0x01 if sampled else 0x00),
                parent.span_id if parent else None, kind, dict(self.resource),
                CLOCK.now(), attributes=dict(attributes or {}), links=list(links or []))
    token = _CURRENT.set(span)                             # this span is now "current"
    try:
        yield span
    except BaseException as exc:                           # record, then re-raise
        span.record_exception(exc)
        raise
    finally:
        span.end_us = CLOCK.now()
        _CURRENT.reset(token)
        if sampled:                                        # unsampled spans never ship
            for exporter in self.exporters:
                exporter.export(span)
```

Crossing a process boundary is a recap of Lesson 3 — format the context into `traceparent`, parse it
back on the far side into a *remote* parent — and the head sampler hashes the trace ID rather than
flipping a coin, which is what makes the decision identical in every service:

```python
def inject(ctx: SpanContext) -> str:
    """version-traceid-spanid-flags  ->  00-4bf9...c31-00f067aa0ba902b7-01"""
    return "00-%s-%s-%02x" % (ctx.trace_id, ctx.span_id, ctx.trace_flags & 0xFF)

class ParentBasedRatioSampler:
    def should_sample(self, parent, trace_id):
        if parent is not None:
            return parent.sampled                             # never split a trace
        return int(trace_id[16:], 16) < self._threshold       # low 8 bytes
```

always the biggest cost** — sum the siblings.

Section 4 is the propagation proof. A root with `sampled=False` writes `-00` into its `traceparent`,
and the downstream service — running in a genuinely empty context, so it can learn nothing except
from the header — creates **0 spans**. The sampled trace produces 2. The decision travelled in one
bit and the whole trace obeyed. The convergence line matters too: the hash is uniform, keeping
**25.3%** of 10,000 trace IDs against a 25% target, which is why you can trust the ratio as a
scaling factor even though any eight consecutive traces look lumpy.

Section 5 is the argument for tail sampling in four numbers. Both policies saw the same 20 traces
containing 3 errors and 3 slow requests. The flat 10% head sample kept **2 of 20 — and 0 of the 3
errors and 0 of the 3 slow ones**. It did exactly what it promised, uniformly, and delivered nothing
you would want at 03:14. The tail policy kept **8 of 20**, including **3/3 errors and 3/3 slow**,
plus 2 boring ones for a latency baseline. Four times the storage, and the entire difference is data
you would actually open. Section 6 closes the funnel: the warning logged from inside
`bank.authorize` carries the same `trace_id` and `span_id` as the orange bar in the waterfall, so
one `grep` moves you between the timing view and the explanation.

## Use It

Real OpenTelemetry Python looks like what you just built. Configure the SDK once at startup, then
use the API everywhere:

```python
from opentelemetry import trace
from opentelemetry.sdk.trace import TracerProvider
from opentelemetry.sdk.trace.export import BatchSpanProcessor
from opentelemetry.sdk.resources import Resource
from opentelemetry.exporter.otlp.proto.grpc.trace_exporter import OTLPSpanExporter

provider = TracerProvider(resource=Resource.create({"service.name": "gateway"}))
provider.add_span_processor(BatchSpanProcessor(OTLPSpanExporter()))  # exports off-thread
trace.set_tracer_provider(provider)
tracer = trace.get_tracer(__name__)

with tracer.start_as_current_span("apply_discount_rules") as span:    # your business logic
    span.set_attribute("cart.item_count", len(items))
    try:
        total = apply(items)
    except Exception as exc:
        span.record_exception(exc)                                    # exception event
        span.set_status(trace.Status(trace.StatusCode.ERROR))
        raise
    span.add_event("discount_applied", {"amount_cents": total.discount})
```

`BatchSpanProcessor` queues finished spans and exports them on a background thread, so a slow
collector cannot add latency to a request. Never use `SimpleSpanProcessor` in production — it
exports synchronously, on the request path. **Zero-code auto-instrumentation** gets the HTTP and
database spans without touching the source at all:

```bash
pip install opentelemetry-distro opentelemetry-exporter-otlp
opentelemetry-bootstrap -a install     # detects your libraries, installs their instrumentations
OTEL_SERVICE_NAME=gateway \
OTEL_EXPORTER_OTLP_ENDPOINT=http://localhost:4317 \
OTEL_TRACES_SAMPLER=parentbased_traceidratio OTEL_TRACES_SAMPLER_ARG=0.1 \
  opentelemetry-instrument python app.py
```

That wrapper installs the SDK before your code imports anything. The FastAPI instrumentation turns
every route into a `SERVER` span with `http.route` and extracts `traceparent` from inbound headers;
`requests`/`httpx` turns every outbound call into a `CLIENT` span and **injects** `traceparent`;
`psycopg` turns every query into a `CLIENT` span carrying `db.query.text`. That is the entire
propagation chain from Lesson 3, for free, in one command. The **Collector** is where the expensive
policy lives, reconfigurable without redeploying anything:

```yaml
receivers:
  otlp:
    protocols: { grpc: { endpoint: 0.0.0.0:4317 }, http: { endpoint: 0.0.0.0:4318 } }
processors:
  tail_sampling:
    decision_wait: 30s          # how long to buffer a trace before judging it
    num_traces: 100000          # in-memory trace capacity — this is your RAM budget
    policies:
      - name: keep-all-errors
        type: status_code
        status_code: { status_codes: [ERROR] }
      - name: keep-slow
        type: latency
        latency: { threshold_ms: 1000 }
      - name: sample-the-rest
        type: probabilistic
        probabilistic: { sampling_percentage: 10 }
  batch: { timeout: 5s, send_batch_size: 1024 }
exporters:
  otlp/tempo: { endpoint: tempo:4317 }
service:
  pipelines:
    traces:
      receivers: [otlp]
      processors: [tail_sampling, batch]     # sample first, then batch what survived
      exporters: [otlp/tempo]
```

Policies are OR-ed: a trace is kept if *any* policy says keep. With more than one collector replica
you must put a `loadbalancing` exporter in front, keyed on `trace_id`, or traces arrive split across
instances and the policies judge fragments. Backends split into two categories — **open source:**
*Jaeger* (the CNCF reference, from Uber), *Grafana Tempo* (object-storage-backed, cheap at volume),
*Zipkin* (the oldest, from Twitter); **hosted:** *Honeycomb* (built around high-cardinality
querying), *Datadog APM*, *Lightstep*. Because they all speak **OTLP**, switching is a Collector
exporter change rather than an instrumentation rewrite — the entire point of the API/SDK split.

Four pieces of operational advice worth months:

- **Turn on auto-instrumentation everywhere first.** One command per service, and it produces the
  propagation graph. Only then add manual `INTERNAL` spans, and only around business logic.
- **Treat `service.name` as sacred.** Set it via `OTEL_SERVICE_NAME` in every deployment. A missing
  or duplicated value silently merges two services in every service map you will ever build.
- **Keep 100% of errors.** Whatever else your sampling does, errors and slow requests are the traces
  you will open. A uniform head sample is a decision to discard the evidence you need.
- **Watch attribute hygiene.** Attributes are indexed: no passwords, tokens, card numbers or
  personal data, and keep span *names* bounded — the cardinality rule from Phase 4 Lesson 5 applies
  to span names exactly as it does to metric labels.

## Think about it

1. A waterfall shows a 900 ms `SERVER` span with a single 40 ms child and nothing else. Where is the
   time, and what are three concrete causes you would check?
2. Your gateway's `CLIENT` span reads 1200 ms while the downstream `SERVER` span for the same call
   reads 60 ms. What is the 1140 ms, and what would you investigate first?
3. You run a 1% head sample and an incident happens at 03:14. What is the probability you have a
   trace of any *specific* failed request — and why does adding a second collector replica break a
   tail-sampling policy that worked with one?
4. A trace has 47 sibling spans named `SELECT product` under one parent. What pattern is that, which
   earlier lesson named it, and would fixing it help if it sits off the critical path?
5. Why must the `sampled` bit travel in `traceparent` rather than each service deciding for itself
   with the same ratio — and what breaks if one service in the chain ignores the bit?

## Key takeaways

- A **span** is a named **interval** — `trace_id`, `span_id`, `parent_span_id`, name, **kind**,
  start/end, **attributes**, **events**, **status**, **links** — and a **trace** is the tree of
  spans sharing a `trace_id`. Only the trace ID, span ID and sampled flag cross a process boundary.
  The design is Google's **Dapper** paper (2010); it replaces the stack trace you lost when you
  split the monolith.
- Read a **waterfall** by shape: long bar with an equally long child means the time is downstream;
  long bar with short children means it is in your code; staggered children mean sequential calls
  that could be concurrent; a gap before the first child means queueing or a lock. Only spans on the
  **critical path** affect total latency — in the build, `bank.authorize` was 96.1% of a 4200 ms
  request while the three database queries were 2.8%.
- **Span kind** (`SERVER`/`CLIENT`/`PRODUCER`/`CONSUMER`/`INTERNAL`) is what lets a backend pair a
  caller with a callee, build a service map, and measure network time — the 8.50 ms gap between the
  matched `CLIENT` and `SERVER` spans in the run.
- **Head sampling** decides at the root by hashing the trace ID (consistent, so every service
  agrees) and propagates one bit; free but blind. **Tail sampling** buffers whole traces in the
  collector and decides on the outcome; it caught **3/3 errors and 3/3 slow traces** where a flat
  10% head sample caught **0/3 and 0/3**, at the cost of RAM and routing every span of a trace to
  the same collector instance.
- **OpenTelemetry** separates the **API** (vendor-neutral, a no-op without an SDK, so libraries can
  instrument themselves), the **SDK** (samplers, batch processors, resource detection), **exporters**
  speaking **OTLP**, and the **Collector** (receivers → processors → exporters) where batching,
  scrubbing and tail sampling belong. **Semantic conventions** make attribute names portable, and
  `service.name` is the one you must never get wrong.
- Start with **auto-instrumentation**, add a few manual spans around business logic, keep 100% of
  errors, and remember the pillars share one key: a span's `trace_id` is the `trace_id` on your logs,
  and metric **exemplars** carry it too — one identifier turns three haystacks into one story.
